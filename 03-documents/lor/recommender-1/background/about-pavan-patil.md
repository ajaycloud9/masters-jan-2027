### Paper 1: Risk, Issues In Promotion Of Brands Through Celebrity Endorsement Strategy

**Journal:** International Journal of Advanced Scientific Research and Management (IJASRM)
**Volume/Issue:** Vol. 4, Issue 3, March 2019
**DOI:** [10.36282/IJASRM/4.3.2019.1284](https://doi.org/10.36282/IJASRM/4.3.2019.1284)
**Full-text PDF:** https://ijasrm.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/03/IJASRM_V4S3_1284_100_104.pdf

**Summary:**

This paper examines the risks and issues that arise when brands promote themselves through celebrity endorsement. While celebrity glamour reliably captures customer attention in an environment where consumers increasingly tune out conventional advertising, the practice also exposes endorsed brands to serious and often unanticipated dangers. The paper identifies six core risk categories:

1. Celebrities overshadowing the brand itself
2. Brands becoming dependent on celebrity identity (the "compulsory evil" effect)
3. Eroding celebrity credibility as consumers grow skeptical of paid endorsements
4. Image mismatch between celebrity persona and brand values
5. Dilution caused by multiple simultaneous endorsements
6. Celebrity scandals and moral violations

The analysis covers eight real-life Indian cases where celebrity endorsers or brand ambassadors faced adverse circumstances that threatened their endorsed brands — including illness (Yuvraj Singh, Lisa Ray, Irrfan Khan, Sonali Bendre), death (Jia Khan), and legal action (Sanjay Dutt, Salman Khan, S. Sreesanth).

The paper concludes that unanticipated personal and professional incidents — including death, disease, and legal action — should be explicitly recognised as a distinct category of risk when planning celebrity endorsement contracts. It also notes that brand ambassadors, who have longer and deeper associative links to brands than one-off endorsers, pose proportionally higher reputational risk. The study is exploratory and qualitative in nature, relying on secondary sources.

### Paper 2: Promoting Brands Through Mascot Endorsers

**Journal:** International Journal of Advanced Scientific Research and Management (IJASRM)
**Volume/Issue:** Vol. 4, Issue 4, April 2019
**DOI:** [10.36282/IJASRM/4.4.2019.1397](https://doi.org/10.36282/IJASRM/4.4.2019.1397)
**Full-text PDF:** http://ijasrm.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/IJASRM_V4S4_1397_196_198.pdf

**Summary:**

This paper explores mascots as a potential alternative endorsement vehicle to real celebrities. While movie actors and sportspersons dominate brand endorsement, the author argues that mascots — fictional characters including illustrated persons, animals, or cartoons — deserve systematic study as endorsers. Unlike real celebrities, marketers must bring mascots into existence themselves, making them fully controllable and free from personal scandal or unpredictable behaviour.

The study is partly motivated by research from social media monitoring firm Synthesio, which found that Pillsbury's Doughboy mascot generated 22.14% of total brand social media buzz, outperforming celebrity endorser Danica Patrick's 12.72% buzz share for GoDaddy.

The paper profiles 11 iconic brand mascots alongside their inception year:

| Brand | Mascot | Year |
|-------|--------|------|
| Michelin | Michelin Man | 1898 |
| Air India | Maharaja | 1946 |
| Asian Paints | Gattu | 1954 |
| Parle-G | Parle-G Girl | 1960s |
| Pillsbury | Doughboy | 1965 |
| Amul | Amul Girl | 1966 |
| McDonald's | Ronald McDonald | 1967 |
| Onida | Devil | 1980s |
| Nirma | Nirma Girl | 1985 |
| PepsiCo 7UP | Fido Dido | 1992 |
| Vodafone | Zoo Zoos | 2009 |

The study finds that mascots have been used to promote both product and service brands for well over a century, and that their usage duration is significantly longer than that of individual celebrity endorsers. The paper concludes that mascots are viable and understudied alternatives — particularly for marketers who cannot afford high-cost celebrity deals — and recommends future research comparing the effectiveness of mascots versus real celebrities across different product categories.

### Paper 3: A Study On Celebrity Endorsed Promotional Video Advertisements With Reference To Jewelers

**Journal:** International Journal of Advanced Scientific Research and Management (IJASRM)
**Volume/Issue:** Vol. 4, Issue 4, April 2019
**DOI:** [10.36282/IJASRM/4.4.2019.1436](https://doi.org/10.36282/IJASRM/4.4.2019.1436)
**Full-text PDF:** https://ijasrm.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/IJASRM_V4S4_1436_373_375.pdf

**Summary:**

This paper analyses how celebrity endorsers are deployed in the promotional video advertisements of jewellery brands. India's deep cultural attachment to gold and ornaments makes women a key target market for jewellers, and female celebrity endorsers are widely used in that space. The study uses McCracken's (1989) framework of four celebrity portrayal modes to evaluate 10 conveniently selected jewellery brand video advertisements:

- **Explicit mode** — the celebrity overtly declares endorsement of the product
- **Implicit mode** — physical or verbal communication is used by the celebrity for the product
- **Imperative mode** — the celebrity suggests viewers to use the product
- **Co-present mode** — the celebrity merely appears alongside the product

Key findings from the analysis of 10 jewellery brands:

- 80% of jewellers used celebrity endorsers in co-present mode
- 10% used implicit mode
- 10% used a combination of implicit and co-present modes
- None used explicit or imperative mode

When measuring the proportion of total video duration in which the celebrity actively appears, the co-present mode ranged from a minimum of just 0–1.66% to a maximum of 80%, revealing that celebrities often occupy only a small fraction of the actual video runtime despite being featured in the advertisement.

The paper concludes that there is significant scope for jewellery marketers to better optimise celebrity usage — both by exploring non-co-present modes (particularly implicit and imperative) and by increasing the proportion of screen time in which the celebrity actively engages with the product. The study is qualitative and exploratory, using secondary data from YouTube videos of selected jewellery brands.
